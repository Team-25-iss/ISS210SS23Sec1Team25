About

Have you ever been part of a school’s sports team? Theater? Debate club? Another after-school activity? Chances are, you have. Extracurricular activities are the highlight of the school day for many students - they’re great opportunities to meet new people, explore new interests and hobbies, and develop valuable interpersonal skills like teamwork. Not to mention how much emphasis college admission officers place on extracurriculars! For them, participation can demonstrate an interest in community involvement and leadership; they can provide a more holistic view of an applicant’s character beyond academic performance. However, there are significant disparities in extracurricular participation - particularly between races and socioeconomic classes. Many activities can be too costly for low-income students to join. Extracurricular cultures can also be hostile to non-white students. These barriers prevent many underserved groups from being able to take full advantage of these opportunities. It is imperative to include extracurricular participation in the conversation when addressing inequity in our schools.

How Racially Diverse Schools and Classrooms Can Benefit All Students

Click here to open this PDF in a new window

The article discusses the benefits of racially and socioeconomically diverse schools and classrooms. It argues that attending these schools has important educational, social, and emotional benefits for all students. The article cites research that shows that students who attend diverse schools are more likely to have higher test scores, be more tolerant of others, and have a better understanding of the world around them. It also argues that these schools can help to close the achievement gap between students of different racial backgrounds. The article concludes by arguing that, as the K-12 student population becomes more diverse, it is important for political leaders to pay attention to the evidence that supports the importance of racially and ethnically diverse educational settings.

The Engagement Gap

Click here to open this PDF in a new window


Regarding socioeconomic class’s relation to education, most discussion revolves around differences in test scores. However, this paper suggests that investigating the differences in extracurricular involvement between social classes is just as important. The authors evaluate statistics from previous studies on this subject and reveal that the disparity between working-class and upper-middle class students’ participation in extracurriculars is growing at an alarming rate.This divide is mostly due to the growing class stratification in general. On a community level, schools in wealthier areas can invest more money into extracurricular programs and can also benefit from philanthropy by community members. This is in contrast to poorer schools that may have to cut back on spending for clubs and sports programs, or be unable to offer them entirely. On an individual level, another issue is the emergence of “pay-to-play” sports programs - ones that require fees to participate in. According to the authors, “one in three sports-playing kids from homes with annual income of $60,000 or less dropped out because of the increased cost, as compared to one in ten kids from families with incomes over $60,000.” This calls for policymakers, parents, and schools to take immediate and urgent action to reduce this disparity in order to reduce educational inequity for our children.

Racism in High School Sports

In this article by the NAACP, the organization makes a pledge to identify racial harassment and outcasting in High School sports and to put a stop to it wherever they see it. It highlights how one black student at Buchanan High school received “monkey/ape noises from a student of the opposing school, and how a Latina student from the same high school was barked at. Both occurring during a penalty kick the victims of the abuse were taking. The article also describes how high school faculty need to take a bigger step in protecting their students from racially motivated abuse.

How America's public schools keep kids in poverty | Kandice Sumner

The video is a TEDx talk by Kandice Sumner. In the talk, Sumner discusses how the intersection of race and class contributes to educational inequality in America. She specifically focuses on how extracurricular activities can exacerbate this inequality, as they often require financial resources that many students from low-income backgrounds don't have. Sumner shares personal anecdotes and research to illustrate her point, arguing that extracurriculars can play a critical role in a student's success, both in school and beyond. She emphasizes that access to extracurricular activities should be a basic right for all students, regardless of their background, and suggests that we need to rethink the way we fund and prioritize these programs. Overall, the talk offers a compelling perspective on the relationship between race, class, and extracurricular participation, and the impact it has on students' lives.

A Quarter Century of Participation in School-Based Extracurricular Activities: Inequalities by Race, Class, Gender and Age?

Click here to open this PDF in a new window

This article mainly describes how current research focuses on describing social differences in youth participation in extracurricular activities, but rarely studies other important differentiating factors, such as race, gender and age, and whether the environment in which the activities take place is in or out of school. In addition, there is a need to explore how these differences influence changes in participation patterns over time and the links between outcomes such as educational attainment, expectations and substance use associated with participation in school-based activities. Therefore, future studies need to consider these factors more comprehensively and use nationally representative historical data to delve deeper into trends and differences.
